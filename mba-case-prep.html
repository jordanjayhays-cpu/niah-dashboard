W21312 PepsiCo: Establishing a Role in a Sustainable Society

How I'd open

"The case isn't really about PepsiCo. It's about the gap between the legitimacy narrative corporations construct and the actual political economy they operate in. Nooyi talked a good game. But PepsiCo spent $3.7M lobbying in 2020 — against sugar taxes, against SNAP improvements, against portion caps. That's not a company trying to solve the problem. That's a company trying to control the narrative while the problem gets worse."

Framework I'd use — Stakeholder Matrix

StakeholderWhat they wantPepsiCo's actual behavior
ConsumersAccurate nutrition infoLobbied against "added sugar" labeling
RegulatorsTransparencyFunded CCF to shift focus to exercise
CommunitiesFood access58% US calories from ultra-processed food
ShareholdersEPS growthCSR used as brand-risk mitigation

Question I'd raise

"If 'Performance with Purpose' is a real strategy — not just repositioning — why does PepsiCo spend more on lobbying against regulation than on product reformulation? You can't simultaneously fund obesity-doubt research and claim to be part of the solution."

My take on the decision

The case frames this as "can PepsiCo go further?" The right question is: can any company in this structural position go further without the regulatory floor being raised beneath them? CSV only works when externalities have costs. Right now, they don't. PepsiCo is optimizing in a system with no price on harm.

R1101C Creating Shared Value — Porter & Kramer (HBR 2011)

How I'd open

"Porter and Kramer are solving a political problem with a business strategy. They're right that Friedmanite shareholder primacy is economically inefficient long-term — but they're assuming the system can self-correct. PepsiCo in W21312 suggests it can't."

The argument I'd challenge

CSV model assumes societal harm = internal cost = incentive to fix it. But:

  • Wal-Mart saves $200M cutting 100M delivery miles → true shared value
  • Nestlé trains African coffee farmers AND secures reliable supply → true shared value
  • PepsiCo claims CSV while lobbying against sugar taxes → CSV theater

My pushback question

"CSV works in supply chains where the company has monopsony-like control — Nestlé over coffee farmers, Unilever over rural India distribution. Does it scale to industries where the structural incentive is to externalize costs? Food processing has structural reasons to keep people eating more, not less. Porter says reconceive products and markets — but what if your core product is the social harm?"

What I'd connect across cases

"Look at PepsiCo and NNF together. Both show CSV-adjacent behavior. PepsiCo's 'Performance with Purpose' failed because the structural incentives ran opposite the narrative. NNF succeeded — temporarily — because the GLP-1 boom was an accidental alignment. Both are fragile. The lesson: CSV is only durable when the ownership structure enforces it. NNF's structure works by accident — when the patent expires, what happens?"

325031 Novo Nordisk Foundation

How I'd open

"This is the most structurally interesting case in the set because it's the only one where the governance mechanism is the subject. NNF owns Novo Nordisk through a foundation structure — and that's both the source of its power and its fundamental vulnerability."

The governance paradox I'd focus on

"NNF is Europe's most powerful philanthropy because of its ownership structure. Foundation-owned firms don't face stock market pressure. They can take 16-year R&D bets. They can sacrifice short-term profit. That's the feature — and also the trap. Their $167B in assets are overwhelmingly NN stock. When GLP-1 patents expire and biosimilars enter, NNF's resource base compresses by 70–80%. The 'wall of money' becomes a wall of dependency."

Question I'd raise — the mission capture problem

"Thomsen ran Novo Nordisk R&D for decades before leading NNF. Does it matter that NNF's leadership is structurally aligned with Novo Nordisk's interests? When a grant review panel evaluates a proposal that might compete with GLP-1s — who flags that conflict? The governance data in Exhibit 3 shows Danish industrial foundations have 99% Danish board members vs. 89% for listed companies. Homogeneous boards + concentrated wealth + pharma adjacency = mission drift."

My sharpest observation

"Compare NNF to Gates Foundation. Gates sells Microsoft stock — diversified. NNF cannot easily sell NN stock without triggering market catastrophe. So NNF is permanently a single-commodity foundation. When that commodity is a $439B market cap built on patent exclusivity — and patent cliffs are real — this 'wealthiest foundation in the world' is a future insolvent philanthropist with $167B in nominal assets and no diversification runway."

The Thread Across All Three

The MBA-level synthesis

"CSV fails when the power structure doesn't enforce it."

W21312 PepsiCo — CEO controls narrative, board controls quarterly earnings → CSV theater
R1101C Porter/Kramer — Framework is right, but assumes good government and good ownership → rarely both
325031 NNF — Structure enforces long-termism → accidentally → but permanently exposed to one stock

The real question isn't whether companies can create shared value. It's whether their ownership and governance structures let them. CSV is a strategy for firms already insulated from short-term capital. For everyone else — PepsiCo, Kraft, Coca-Cola — it's a marketing budget. The cases together tell us: you can't fix a broken structure with a better narrative.
